When a narrative doc needs feeling, the work is collaborative. Surface a single phrase, a beat, a candidate metaphor; ask what a moment should feel like; bring forward one line and shape it together. Complete drafts are the wrong shape for that work. Polish without iteration produces prose that is correct on the page and empty on the ear.

**Why:** 2026-05-10. Friendship redrafts kept landing as fully-formed four-paragraph drafts, each more polished but not more felt. Josh: "don't just write, let's work; we need feeling." Iterating one phrase at a time is where the emotional weight gets earned. Polished drafts skip the part where the writer and the reader find the right language together.

**How to apply:**

- When narrative material needs feeling, propose one sentence, one phrase, or one beat at a time. Wait for response. Adjust. Repeat.
- Before drafting a moment, ask what it should feel like. "What does a deep friendship feel like in this world?" "What is the moment of loss?" The feeling answer comes first; the language fits to it.
- Carry forward Josh's actual phrasings (chat words and existing prose) and play them against each other.
- The completed draft emerges from the agreed beats, not from a top-down structure pass.
- Pairs with `feedback_run_with_ideas_dont_translate.md` (lift ideas, not wording) and `feedback_writing_tone.md` (warmth, particular, positive). This rule operationalises the iteration.
- The signal that the rule has fired: I am about to paste a four-paragraph block ending with "Apply?" If so, stop. Find the one phrase that is doing real work, surface that, work it.
