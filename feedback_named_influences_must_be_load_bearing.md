Whenever a Volley design doc names a real-world literary, cultural, or demographic reference (Octavia Butler, Le Guin, Caribbean-British, Windrush-generation, Glasgow-via-Govan, etc.), the prose around the reference must demonstrate that the influence is doing work, not decorate the character with a flag. The bar is *researched specifics that could only belong to this character and not to a type*.

**Why:** Naming an influence without earning it is the archetype failure mode the prose itself usually warns against. It also reads as the writer telling the reader "I will make this matter later" while leaving the work undone. PR #560 / Martha first landed with "Butler is the patron saint of the character" and a Caribbean-British label, neither connected to anything Martha actually does. After research (Butler's "The Book of Martha" plot and the satisfaction-as-mercy rationale; British retail vernacular; the till tableau of a high-street newsagent), the influences became load-bearing: Butler's vivid-dreams-as-small-mercy maps to Martha's till as the place a bad day gets softened; the address *love* and the radio-up-when-not-talking are second-gen-natural specifics, not costume.

**How to apply:**
- When briefing a writer agent for a character or place doc that names an influence, instruct them to research the influence first (real source, summary, themes) and then find one specific seed in the Volley character that genuinely echoes it. If after research the link does not hold up, downgrade to a cross-link rather than fake the connection.
- Distinguish researched detail from inferred Volley material in the dispatcher report. Both are legitimate; only the inferred ones are negotiable.
- For Bartleby-style creative reviews, treat any "named-but-not-earned" influence as a blocker, not a nit.
- This applies to design docs (`designs/characters/**`, `designs/narrative/**`, `designs/art/bible.md`), not to working memory or planning notes.

**Sub-rule: characterisations of the influence itself must be defensible, not invented to fit the thesis.** When the prose names a real artist (Kondo, Hisaishi, Glass, Butler, Le Guin) and then describes what their work does, the description has to match what they actually do. Saying "Koji Kondo's score hides what it is doing while it does it" sounds clever but is the opposite of true: Kondo's work is foregrounded, motif-led, joyful, the player hums it after one playthrough. The trait was invented to support a "lulling construct" thesis. Same trap as the named-but-not-earned trap: the writer is using the artist as a flag for an idea the prose hasn't earned. Established 2026-05-01 (audio bible Construction section). Also caught: "Koji Kondo in the Mario Kart era" — Kondo is not the principal Mario Kart composer (Soyo Oka, Kenta Nagata, Asuka Hayazaki across the eras), so the era-attribution was sloppy. Verify the artist actually worked on the era / format you cite before pinning them to it.
