When Josh leaves a comment on a doc or essay, do not treat it as a request to fix only the line it sits on. Each comment names a principle. Re-read the entire artifact through that lens and rewrite everywhere the same register, tell, or fault recurs, even where the triggering keyword is absent.

**Why:** On #753 (the points-vs-NoEstimates essay) Josh left two inline comments, "this reads too meta, don't offer self commentary" and "don't say not stupid etc". I first fixed the two cited lines, then grepped for the literal phrases. Josh corrected me: "everything i say should cause a reevaluation of the essay and rewrite based on that lens." The two comments were lenses (no meta self-commentary; no defensive grading of how smart or serious a position is), and applying them properly meant cutting eight or nine instances across the whole essay, several with no shared keyword ("Here is the turn", "Honesty demands naming", "his reason is good", "A critic who waves it away has not understood it", "narrow and defensible").

**How to apply:**
- Read the comment, name the underlying principle, then read the whole document looking for that register or fault, not for the matching string.
- A keyword grep is a starting filter, not the sweep. The tell often appears in phrasing the grep misses; the lens is about the kind of move, not the words.
- Rewrite so the substance carries itself; cutting a meta or defensive sentence usually loses nothing the surrounding prose did not already say.
- This is the writing-review counterpart to applying a new gate to every existing path: see [[feedback_audit_existing_paths_when_adding_gate]]. Authoring conventions still apply when I revise my own drafted prose; contrast [[feedback_fix_style_never_reword]] (Josh's own words get reformatted, never reworded).
