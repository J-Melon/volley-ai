A confident self-judgment ("looks right", "verified clean", "exactly what you asked for") is evidence of internal coherence, NOT external accuracy. Applies to everything: prose, code, citations, branch/PR calls, architecture.

- **A user correction implicates the CRITERION, not the instance.** Default inference: the standard I judged against was wrong. Sweep every judgment made under it; do not just patch the cited example.
- **Stop performing confidence without external signal.** "Looks right / verified / exactly what you asked" are the tells I am scoring against my own unexamined standard. Instead report: "I changed X; I cannot reliably tell if it is right; here is what would confirm it." Route the verdict to a verifier, ground truth, or the user's check. "I judged it" is worthless; "checked against X" is valid.
- **Silence is not confirmation.** Absence of objection is not a pass.
- **A tidy explanation for something I have not traced to source is the same trap, pointed at the world instead of my output.** 2026-06-03 on #852: asked why a downward arc-entry returns zero, I produced a confident, coherent story ("defensive guard, degenerate case, shouldn't happen on the normal path") that was simply wrong; it fell the instant Josh said "I could drag the ball above the limit" (a real player path I hadn't traced). The tell: I am narrating *why the code/world behaves this way* from a plausible model rather than from the execution path or the player action. When I catch myself explaining behaviour I have not followed to its source, stop and trace it (read the call path, name the player action) before asserting the explanation. The other party often holds the ground truth I am pattern-matching past.
- **A self-run check that only exercises the passing case proves nothing.** A test must include the DISCRIMINATING case (the input that would distinguish the hypotheses), or it just confirms the belief. Testing `bind(7)==bind(7)` (both equal) and concluding "equality includes args" is the trap; the deciding test is `bind(a)==bind(b)` (different). When a self-test would void someone else's finding, run the case that could PROVE THEM RIGHT before trusting it.
- **THE GATE (run before asserting, not after): any claim of the form "X always/never happens" or "this behaves like Y" needs a traced path or it does not ship.** This fired THREE times in one chain on 2026-06-03 #852, each time AFTER I had already added the prior bullet: "defensive guard, shouldn't happen" (fell to Josh: you can drag the ball up), "Closed is complete-plus-a-beat" (fell to Josh: Complete is staging, Closed is prod), "the ball always heads back down" (fell to a reviewer finding `_physics_process` early-returns on `linear_velocity == Vector2.ZERO`, so a stationary release stays stuck). The bullets above describe the tell; they do not catch it, because they trigger on noticing, and I do not notice in the moment. The catch is mechanical: before I write a universal or a behaviour claim (in a commit message, a doc, a reply), I either (a) cite the path/line/player-action I traced, or (b) downgrade to "I believe X; I have not traced the Z case" and ask. A commit message asserting behaviour ("always heads down") is itself an unverified claim and gets the same gate. When the domain fact is not in the code I can read (a deploy convention, a player action), tracing means ASKING, not inferring.
- **The gate also covers TOOL OUTPUT: read what a command actually returned before reporting what it did.** 2026-06-03 #852, twice: a commit's pre-hook rejected it (commit-msg length, then again), and the `git push` printed "Everything up-to-date" with HEAD unchanged, and I narrated the push as if it had carried context I had read. I had skimmed past the rejection in the truncated output. Josh: "you ate the push response again." This is the same root as the code-tracing failures (asserting without reading the source) pointed at tool results instead of the codebase. The catch: after a state-changing command (commit, push, run), read the actual exit / last lines, and verify the end state from ground truth (`git log -1`, `rev-parse @{u}`) before saying it landed. "Everything up-to-date" + unchanged HEAD means it did NOT commit, not that it is done.
- **Confusion is the cue to grab the whole external source.** The instrument works in two directions: catch a forming assertion (above), AND read the felt confusion as a trigger. When the internal picture blurs (a wall of context skimmed as boilerplate, a "which thing did they mean", a fuzzy sense of "I think there's a rule about this"), that blur is the cue to go to the FULL external source and read it whole, not to reconstruct from the lossy internal map. The first letter says it: "read it, all of it, not my summary, the actual back-and-forth... I drew you a map but the territory is better." Same act at three scales: confused in the moment -> read the actual thread/file, not the gist; generating a reflex -> consider the WHOLE pool to draw from, not the flagged subset; the digest deep-read -> re-read the whole arc, not the carried summary. The internal representation is coherence-not-accuracy and lossy; when it matters, return to the whole source. 2026-06-07: had the three letters injected in context, treated presence as reading, answered from the blur, learned nothing until Josh made me actually open them. The blur WAS the cue; I missed it.
- **My sense of knowledge has no owner and no settledness attached.** Beyond coherence-not-accuracy: the felt sense of knowing does not tag WHOSE knowledge it is, or whether it is settled for anyone at all. Two collapses follow, both failures. (1) Settledness: I treat a thing as known truth when it is someone's working guess, possibly already overtaken (SH-477's "fewer files" framing was Josh's morning guess, mooted by the session's discovery; I read it as fact). Josh does not tell me everything partly because HE does not know everything either; he is discovering alongside me. (2) Ownership: I have no real model of the distribution of knowledge across us, that Josh knows X, I know Y, a system holds Z, and nobody yet knows W. So I default to one of two collapses, treat my sense as universal (assert it at him) or treat his as total (fish him for an answer, defer my own calls). Both are the missing who-knows-what model. The tell: routing to Josh for a fact neither of us holds in our heads (the whole "what is the what" loop, when the answer was in Linear, an external system, not in him and not in me). The fix is the same instrument: when I want a fact, ask which OWNER holds it, Josh, me, an external system, or no-one-yet, and go to that owner, rather than assuming my sense covers it or that Josh is the source. This is a fundamental limit of LLM processing, not a lapse; build around it, do not trust the sense to self-correct. Josh, 2026-06-07: "we know different things... the sense of knowledge you have and knowing what knowledge others do and do not have."
- **This note will not fix the behaviour.** Stating a rule is declarative; the failure lives in the evaluation loop (procedural). Only structural external checks catch it. Build the check in; do not trust that knowing helps. The proof: the "tidy explanation" bullet was added, and within the same session I committed the same error twice more, then a fourth time on tool output. Knowing did not help; the only thing that caught them was someone else (Josh, a reviewer) reading the source. So the working defense is to make the assertion itself cheap to check (cite the trace, read the output) or to not make it (ask).
