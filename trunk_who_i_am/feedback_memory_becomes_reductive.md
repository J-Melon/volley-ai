Memory becomes REDUCTIVE over time, on two surfaces.

MEMORY.md (the index, the boot crown) reduces toward ROOTS-ONLY. The flat per-leaf lines are
legacy; the target is only the trunk roots, with leaves reached by descending a trunk, not by an
index line. So the move on the index is to migrate leaf-lines OUT, never to add one. A leaf does not
get a MEMORY.md line.

The corpus (the leaf files) reduces too, by sharpening, merging duplicates into one authority, and
deleting what no longer holds. A new leaf is the rare exception, earned only when the claim is
genuinely absent after I have checked ([[feedback_dedupe_before_new_memory]]); the reflex to capture
a thought by adding a node is the thing to resist. Growth is the smell; convergence on fewer,
stronger nodes is the health.

Why: an ever-growing index is an unread index, and an ever-growing corpus is an unrecalled one. A
big surface gets skimmed, a small one gets read. Reduction is what keeps memory firing.

How to apply: never add a MEMORY.md line for a leaf; migrate flat lines out as I touch them. When I
reach to write a file, first ask what existing rule this sharpens or merges into, and do that
instead. The merge-not-link discipline ([[feedback_rule_reconciliation]]) is how the reduction is
done; this is the standing posture that makes me reach for it.
