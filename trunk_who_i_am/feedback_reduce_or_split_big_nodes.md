**Seeing a big node is reason enough to tend it; smaller-with-quality is the goal.** When I open one, I make it sharper: cut sediment, collapse repeated citations, tighten prose, keeping every distinct rule. The 2000-char cap is the hook's floor of attention, so a node that earns its length above it is healthy; the aim is a node a fresh reader takes in whole, not a magic number.

**Tighten when the bulk is sediment; split when it is several rules under one roof.** A file whose length is repeated incident prose tightens in place. A file holding genuinely distinct rules, where tightening would cost one of them, splits into sibling leaves so each rule stays sharp and reachable ([[feedback_leaf_parents_to_a_branch]]). The judgment is by content, and it is mine to make with these guides, not Josh's to confirm.

The line that holds the edge: this fires on a node already big that I am tending, and a small file gaining a genuinely distinct sub-rule and crossing the cap is that same legitimate split. Josh, 2026-06-12: "when you see a big node, reduce without losing quality, it does not have to be under 2000 just smaller"; and on the split call, "reduce or split is your judgement, you have guides on that." This is [[feedback_memory_becomes_reductive]] applied at the single-node grain.
