When a Volley essay reads unclear about what it is saying, the instinct (and the conventional writing advice) is to STATE THE THESIS earlier and harder. For Volley voice that is WRONG. The load-bearing claim is a THEME the reader assembles from the evidence, the way a theme emerges from a novel, never from its blurb.

**Why:** The "I Am Not A Robot" essay (2026-05-30) read unclear. Josh's diagnosis was that the central claim (the oldest question, what is mind, has been quietly taken over and shrunk to whatever can be measured and shipped) was not landing. The first fix attempt briefed an agent to "make the claim legible and surface it early"; the agent planted "That substitution is the move this essay is about... a rebranding operation" and "has been rebranded as an engineering problem". Josh: "don't let them say that outright, this is a THEME." Both lines were reverted. The real fix was CONVERGENCE: every section enacts the same move so consistently the reader cannot conclude anything else, with the claim never named.

**How to apply:**
- An essay is unclear because its sections do not all visibly bend toward one move, NOT because the claim is unstated. Fix the convergence, do not add an announcement.
- Banned in the prose: the theme as a sentence; meta-sentences about the essay ("this essay argues", "the move here is"); the briefing-words for the theme (here: rebrand/takeover/hostile). The word WE use to brief must never appear in the text.
- Make what the question/stake ACTUALLY is vivid and large, so its shrinking is FELT by contrast. The contrast carries the claim; the prose never states it.
- The close lets the reader land on the theme themselves. Implication, not declaration.
- This is show-don't-tell ([[feedback_describe_before_naming]]) at the scale of the whole argument, and the anti-signposting rule applied to the thesis itself.
