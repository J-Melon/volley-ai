When Josh says "let's dissect this", "what does X add", "I want to focus on getting you to understand", the goal is judgment, not output. Responding with a quick fix proposal short-circuits the learning he is trying to set up.

**Sharpened 2026-05-15** during the gear narrative iteration on Operation Marginalia. Josh asked "what does [closing sentence] add to the doc?" I correctly identified that it added nothing factual, then immediately offered "want me to land that" as a fix. Josh: "Stop trying to fix it, I want to focus on getting you to understand."

**Why:** the fix without the understanding leaves me re-making the same class of mistake on the next iteration. Josh has cut the same shape of decoration multiple times in this thread; the pattern was the lesson, not any individual sentence.

**How to apply:**
- Recognise the shape: "let's dissect / what does X do / what is X / why did you do X" → reasoning prompt, not action prompt.
- Respond with: what was wrong about my reasoning that produced X, what general pattern this is an instance of, what the principle is. Use plain language.
- Do not append "want me to fix it / land it / revise it" to the response. The fix comes later if at all.
- Substantive carry from this case: every sentence in a world-bible doc must add a fact (definition / mechanism / constraint / picture). Flourish is rare and earned; "earned" means the sentence carries information no other sentence does. Literary-essay closure (land on a loaded image) does NOT earn its place in a world-bible.
