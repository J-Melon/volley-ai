STYLE.md's "Prose rhythm: where the ear lands" gives plosive-ending / end-stress rules. They are a CHECK to catch a close that genuinely drifts (a Latinate or prepositional or weak-pronoun ending where the sentence wanted to land hard). They are NOT a target to retro-fit every paragraph onto.

**Why:** In the "I Am Not A Robot" essay (2026-05-30), the cadence pass restructured EIGHTEEN paragraph closes to plosive endings in one go. Three broke meaning: the essay's deliberate final word "progress" (the labs' own word for the evasion, the whole irony of the close) became "a fact" for a /t/; "the schedule is fiction" (a load-bearing thesis line and a boundary echo) became "made up" for a /p/, also breaking its echo with the next section's opening; "the gap is in the signal" (which sets up the radio-telescope metaphor that follows) became "what was never broadcast", pre-empting the metaphor. The plosive rule served itself instead of the argument, and eighteen hard closes in a row is a new monotony, the exact "device on autopilot" the voice skill warns against.

**How to apply:**
- Brief a cadence pass to FLAG drifting closes and fix only those, not to maximise plosive endings. Expect a handful of changes in a tight essay, not one per paragraph.
- A deliberate word choice (a chosen final noun, a load-bearing phrase, a metaphor set-up, a boundary echo) OUTRANKS the phonetic rule. Meaning first; sound serves it.
- Uniform application of any one device (plosive closes, anaphora, tricolon) reads as performance, not thought. The voice skill says at most one device per paragraph, often none; the same restraint applies to cadence retrofitting.
- After a cadence pass, re-read for closes that got hard at the cost of the right word, and revert those. Relates to [[feedback_review_comment_is_a_lens]] (a rule is a lens to sweep, not a mechanical line-fix).
