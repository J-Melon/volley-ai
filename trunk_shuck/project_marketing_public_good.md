**Every Volley marketing action puts money or time in real places.** The principle is *civic* action: charity events, in-person game days, tournaments where the proceeds go somewhere real, merch collaborations with small craftspeople or illustrators or printers who get a meaningful cut, sponsorship of community fundraisers, hosting space at events the studio can lift attention onto. The studio is always in a position to show up alongside a person or a group of someones, and the marketing happens because the showing-up happens.

**Watch the language.** Avoid framing the work as "the help" or "helping people." That language othering-positions the people on the receiving end and reads as the giver standing above them. Use specific verbs: *supports*, *shows up for*, *partners with*, *runs with*, *backs*. Use specific nouns: *the partnership*, *the work*, *the contribution*, *the action*. Charity money is charity money; the studio names the charity and what was raised, not "the help we gave."

**Distinguish this from the open-development thesis.** Open repo, devlogs, postmortems, free tools, free score cuts under a permissive licence: those are the [open-development essay's](../../../gamedev/volley/designs/research/the-case-for-open-development.md) thesis. They are valuable and the studio does them too. Marketing-as-public-good is a separate axis: real people in the same room as the studio's money or time, where the marketing surface points at the work honestly. The two complement each other but the distinction holds.

**Cheap and guerrilla over expensive and conventional.** Marketing budget goes to the work itself (the charity donation, the venue rental, the merch print run with the small collaborator), rather than to amplifying it through paid ads, paid influencer placements, or wishlist-campaign-shaped marketing. The amplification comes from the work being good.

**How to apply:**
- When ticketing or briefing marketing work, the first question is "where, specifically, does this put money or time?" If the answer is "the audience, by being given a free thing", that is the open-development axis (still good, but separate). If the answer is "this charity, this group of local players, this small printer", that is the marketing-as-public-good axis.
- Examples of the right shape: a Volley-themed tournament where entry fees go to a UK youth tennis charity; a Saturday at a local racquet-sport club the studio sponsors; a £20 enamel pin done with a one-person Edinburgh enamel-pin maker, split-revenue or full-margin-to-them; a postcard set with a small illustrator who keeps the original prints to sell separately; a pop-up at an indie games event where the booth-space hosts smaller devs alongside Volley.
- Examples of the wrong shape (paid amplification dressed up as goodwill): a "$1 per copy sold during launch week" donation, joining a charity bundle for visibility rather than for the cause, a sponsored stream where the streamer gets paid and the charity is incidental.
- The charity, cause, or collaborator should be one the studio can name and would back anyway, chosen for the cause itself rather than the optics.
- This applies at every band of the funding matrix. At Band 0 the work is small-money or time-only. At Band 5 it is studio-scale (sponsored programmes, sustained partnerships, real budget).
- Let the action carry the message. The studio talks about the work in concrete terms ("we ran X with Y so Z group could afford to compete"), framed around the partnership and the contribution, not around self-congratulation.
- **Cap community fundraising matching at a fixed amount per band.** A viral charity stream could raise sums that bankrupt the studio if the matching pledge is uncapped. When the studio amplifies a community fundraiser, either (a) amplify only (host the stream, lend the platform, donate keys), or (b) pledge a *fixed cap* (e.g. £100 / £500 / £5K depending on band). Match-uncapped is a category error: the upside is unbounded exposure for a marketing benefit that is fixed. Scaled charities know this; small charities sometimes ask for it. If asked, decline politely and counter-offer a capped pledge.
