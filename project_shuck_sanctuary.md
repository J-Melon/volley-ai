Shuck (the studio behind Volley!) positions itself as a sanctuary from the toxicity of game development, a place where artists and contributors can work on craft without the usual industry pressure, command culture, or instability.

**Why:** the game industry's baseline is crunch, top-down command, and transactional collaboration. Shuck was founded to be different, and the framing of its work should show that difference, not just claim it.

**How to apply:**

- Describe rules, standards, and design targets as **invitations or descriptions**, not commands. The art bible's Six Marks (Intentional, Specific, Distinct, Diegetic, Breathing, Shifting) are called Marks for this reason, not commandments. They describe what the work looks like when it is landing; they do not command anyone to do anything.
- Replace imperative framings in public docs with descriptive ones where possible. "You must X" → "X is what it looks like when the work is working." "Contributors are required to" → "contributors are invited to."
- Preserve this framing in new docs and memoranda that establish design standards, contribution expectations, or team culture. Shuck's word for what it asks of people is invitation, not command.
- If an older doc uses command-register phrasing (commandment, must, obey, govern, rule), consider softening when updating.
- The specific phrase "commandments" was imported from Lorne Lanning / Oddworld as shorthand. Shuck's equivalent is **the Six Marks** (capitalised as a proper term) in the art bible; do not reintroduce "commandment" framing.
